\section{Introduction to Differential Calculus}

\emph{Differential} has the same root as difference and this class, \emph{differential calculus}, is about calculating differences. Put another way, this class is really about subtraction.

More specifically, the main question of differential calculus is how to approximate the change in a funtion's output by scaling a small change in its input. The \emph{scaling factor} that gives the best approximation is the \emph{derivative}.

You've seen scaling factors before. Look at a map of Seattle, for example, and you'll see a scale, maybe 3 miles/inch. But for a larger scale map, like one of the earth, there is no global scale like this. Instead, the scaling factor varies from place to place and is usually a function of latitude. Even at a specific location, there is often not one, but infinitely many scaling factors. Each factor depends on direction. 

But for a \emph{conformal} map (from the Latin \emph{conformalis}, \emph{of the same shape}), where the scaling factor does not depend on direction. These maps preserve angles. The most well-know is probably the Mercator map. But we'll start the course with another.
